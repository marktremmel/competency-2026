The framework behind the numbers
What the Országos kompetenciamérés (OKM) actually measures, who builds it, and why one year's score can be compared with the next — a plain-language companion to the data.

🎯 The idea behind the measurement

The Országos kompetenciamérés (National Competency Measurement, OKM) looks back on roughly two decades of history and is now an organic part of the Hungarian public-education system. Every year, on the last Wednesday of May, students sit reading-comprehension and mathematics tests, fill in background questionnaires, and receive school reports built from the results.

Crucially, it has never been a test of memorised facts. From the start it was designed to examine problem-solving placed in realistic situations — whether a student can find, select, judge and apply the right information.

Its primary aim is to give every school the objective indicators it needs for self-evaluation and for setting development priorities — while also supporting maintainers, external evaluation, families, and evidence-based education policy.

🏛️ Who assembles it, and why

The measurements and their content frameworks are produced by the Oktatási Hivatal (the Hungarian Education Authority), Budapest. The current digital frameworks were developed within a major EU-funded project (EFOP-3.2.15-VEKOP), issued under responsible publisher Brassói Sándor and written by named teams of subject specialists.

A single national authority defines what is measured and how — one common scale, one set of definitions, one standardised procedure for every school — so a result means the same thing wherever and whenever it was measured.
The content framework (tartalmi keret)
A tartalmi keret is the published blueprint for a measured area. For each subject it sets out the content areas (what knowledge is in scope), the thinking operations (what the student does with it), and the test matrix that combines them. Every item sits at one intersection of the two.

💻 Why digital — and adaptive

Finalised in its current form around 2010, the measurement ran on paper for a decade and moved onto computers from 2022. Because everyday reading and problem-solving now happen in digital environments, the test medium was brought closer to what it measures: more objective automated scoring, equal-opportunity built-in tools, and richer process data (time-on-task, revisits).

Adaptive testing. As a student answers, an algorithm estimates their ability and serves task blocks matched to it. Same grade, same item bank, but personalised versions — so the weakest and strongest students are measured far more reliably than with a one-size-fits-all paper test.

📈 How — and why — years compare

Every student gets a score on one national scale (~1100–2100). The same scale is used every year and across grades, so the number itself is the unit of comparison: a given score means the same ability whether earned in Grade 6 or 8, in 2022 or 2026.

The continuous score is cut into seven competency levels anchored to that scale (see any tile — click for the level descriptions). When the system went digital the organisers deliberately kept the scale, allowing only gradual content change — the core volume is literally titled "Folytonosság és változás" (Continuity and change). Hungary's PISA, TIMSS and PIRLS participation follows the same logic.

About the Project & Methodology
Context, descriptions of statistical metrics, data sources, and pedagogical philosophy.

Project Purpose

This dashboard turns the raw 2026 National Competency Measurement (OKM) score files into something school leadership and teachers can act on. Instead of stopping at a single school average, it lets you read each group's score distribution, compare against peer schools across Budapest, and follow a cohort's multi-year trajectory.

The aim is practical: spot the groups that need support early — such as the Grade 4–5 reading and numeracy gaps — so attention and resources reach them in time.

It is one of three connected pieces: this interactive dashboard for exploring, a printable PDF report for leadership, and the OKM Framework tab explaining what the measurement is and why scores compare across years.

Pedagogical Philosophy: Data vs. Human Reality

Is this just overreliance on data? In education, data should be a compass, not a verdict. Test scores tell us where a gap exists, but never why. A low reading comprehension average in Grade 4 flags a structural concern, but it does not measure a student's curiosity, resilience, or potential.

Understanding Fluctuations: Student populations naturally vary. Small cohorts (such as Grade 11 with only 8–9 students) are highly sensitive to statistical noise. A single student having a bad day or missing the exam can swing the average by 50+ points. That is why this dashboard places heavy emphasis on cohort size, absenteeism, and distribution spreads rather than simple means.

How to use it: Use the aggregate data to guide school-wide resource allocation, and use individual student tracking (via the spreadsheet tool) to support differentiated classroom instruction.

Key Statistical Metrics Explained

SD (Standard Deviation / Szórás)

Measures the absolute spread of scores around the class average. A low SD (<100) means students perform evenly (homogeneous group). A high SD (>170) indicates wide achievement gaps between the top and bottom students (heterogeneous group), requiring differentiated teaching.

CV% (Coefficient of Variation / Relatív szórás)

Calculated as (SD / Mean) * 100. By expressing standard deviation as a percentage of the mean, it normalizes the spread. This allows direct comparison of student diversity across different grades and subjects, regardless of their scaling differences.

CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate / Összetett éves növekedési ütem)

The smoothed average annual growth rate over the historical years: ((Mean_2026 / Mean_Start) ** (1 / (2026 - Year_Start)) - 1) * 100. It smooths out annual fluctuations to show the long-term, multi-year developmental trajectory of the cohort. (Hungarian: éves átlagos növekedési ütem.)

At-risk rate

Share of students below the Minimum level (Levels 1–2). The single most important early-warning number — it counts the pupils who have not yet reached an acceptable standard.

vs National (point gap)

School average minus the national average for the same group, in scale points — positive (green) means ahead of the country, negative (red) behind. The 2026 national figures are not released yet, so comparisons use the most recent national data available.
